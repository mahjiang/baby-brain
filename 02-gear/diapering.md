## Cloth vs Disposable

| Factor | Disposable | Cloth |
|--------|-----------|-------|
| Convenience | Easy, no laundry | Requires washing routine |
| Cost (first year) | ~$900–$1,200 (diapers + wipes + supplies) | ~$400–$800 upfront + ~$100–$200/yr laundry |
| Cost (2.5 yrs to potty training) | ~$2,000–$2,800 | ~$500–$1,000 total (reuse for 2nd child = nearly free) |
| Environmental | ~4,000–6,000 diapers to landfill; 500+ yrs to decompose | Water/energy for washing; 25% less CO2 with best practices |
| Skin health | Modern disposables wick moisture well; may contain trace chemicals (dioxins, phthalates) | Natural fibers (cotton, bamboo); more breathable; no chemical concerns |
| Apartment factor | Need diaper pail; easy trash disposal | Need storage + washer access; drying space |
| Daycare compatibility | Universal acceptance | Many daycares won't accept cloth |
| Our situation | Simpler for apartment life | Possible if in-unit laundry; harder without |

### Evidence Summary

*Added 2026-02-28*

**Cost:** Families spend $70–$100/month on disposable diapers, totaling ~$900–$1,200 in the first year including wipes and cream. Cloth diapers cost $400–$800 upfront for a full stash plus accessories, with ongoing laundry costs of ~$100–$200/year. Cloth savings increase significantly if reused for a second child. (Source: [TrustedCare Diapering Costs](https://trustedcare.com/costs/diapering-costs))

**Environmental:** The UK Environment Agency's lifecycle assessment (updated 2008, revisited 2023) found that cloth diapers produce 25% less CO2 than disposables when parents follow best practices: wash at 60C, line dry, and reuse for subsequent children. Without those practices, the environmental impact is roughly similar. Disposable diapers are among the top consumer items in landfills. (Source: [UK DEFRA Lifecycle Assessment](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/an-updated-lifecycle-assessment-for-disposable-and-reusable-nappies); [Bayrli Environmental Impact](https://bayrli.com/blogs/discover/cloth-diapers-vs-disposable-environmental-impact))

**Skin health:** The AAP recommends zinc oxide and/or petrolatum-based creams for diaper rash prevention regardless of diaper type. Modern disposables have good moisture-wicking but may contain trace chemicals. Cloth diapers use natural fibers but require more frequent changes. Evidence is mixed on which causes more rash — frequency of changes matters more than diaper type. (Source: [Mayo Clinic Diaper Rash](https://www.mayoclinic.org/diseases-conditions/diaper-rash/diagnosis-treatment/drc-20371641); [PMC Study on Cloth vs Disposable](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11889673/))

> **Decision:** TBD — discuss with partner. Key question: do you have in-unit laundry? If yes, cloth is viable and saves money long-term. If no, disposable is the practical choice for apartment life.

## Cloth Diaper Types (If Going Cloth)

*Added 2026-02-28*

| Type | How It Works | Pros | Cons | Price Each |
|------|-------------|------|------|-----------|
| **Pocket** | Waterproof shell + stuffable insert pocket | Customizable absorbency, dries fast, stay-dry feel | Must stuff before use, remove insert before wash | $8–$25 |
| **All-in-One (AIO)** | Single piece, everything sewn together | Easiest to use (like a disposable), no assembly | Expensive, slow to dry | $20–$35 |
| **All-in-Two (AI2) / Covers** | Reusable waterproof cover + removable insert | Cheapest system, cover reused between changes, fast drying | Slightly more steps at change time | $5–$15 cover + $3–$8/insert |
| **Prefolds + Covers** | Flat absorbent cloth folded into cover | Cheapest option, very durable, fast drying | Steepest learning curve | $2–$4 prefold + $10–$15 cover |

### Best Cloth Brands

- **Budget:** Mama Koala (6-packs with bamboo inserts, ~$8/diaper), Alva Baby (hundreds of prints, great shells, ~$6/diaper)
- **Mid-range:** bumGenius, Thirsties, Charlie Banana (~$15–$20/diaper)
- **Premium:** Kanga Care Rumparooz (~$25/diaper)

**For apartments:** Pocket diapers or AI2/cover systems are best — inserts separate from covers so everything dries faster (important without a dedicated laundry room or outdoor line). AIOs take longest to dry and are least apartment-friendly. ## Diaper Rash Prevention & Cream

*Added 2026-02-28*

### Evidence-Based Prevention

The AAP and AAD recommend:
- Change diapers frequently (every 2-3 hours, immediately after bowel movements)
- Allow air-dry time between changes when possible
- Use a barrier cream with **zinc oxide** and/or **petrolatum** as active ingredient
- Choose fragrance-free, alcohol-free, dye-free products
- Avoid talc/baby powder (aspiration risk, no proven benefit)

(Source: [Mayo Clinic Diaper Rash Treatment](https://www.mayoclinic.org/diseases-conditions/diaper-rash/diagnosis-treatment/drc-20371641); [Consumer Reports Diaper Cream Guide](https://www.consumerreports.org/babies-kids/diaper-creams/how-to-choose-a-diaper-cream-without-harmful-chemicals-a3949764047/))

### Cream Recommendations

| Product | Active Ingredient | Price | Notes |
|---------|-------------------|-------|-------|
| **Desitin Maximum Strength** | 40% zinc oxide | ~$7–$10 | Thick paste, best for active rash |
| **Desitin Daily Defense** | 13% zinc oxide | ~$7–$9 | Lighter, good for daily prevention |
| **Aquaphor Baby Healing Ointment** | Petrolatum-based | ~$8–$12 | Great daily barrier, not zinc-based |
| **A+D Original Ointment** | Petrolatum + lanolin | ~$7–$10 | Classic daily protectant |
| **Boudreaux's Butt Paste** | 16% zinc oxide | ~$8–$10 | Popular middle-ground option |
| **Earth Mama Organics** | Organic herbs + zinc | ~$10–$14 | Cloth-diaper safe, clean ingredients |

**Recommendation:** Get two products: **Aquaphor Baby** for daily barrier use at every change, and **Desitin Maximum Strength** to have on hand for active rash. If using cloth diapers, use **Earth Mama Organics** or another cloth-safe cream (standard zinc oxide pastes can cause buildup on cloth fibers).

> **Important for cloth diapers:** Most zinc oxide creams require a liner between cream and cloth diaper, or they'll cause repelling/buildup. Look for creams specifically labeled "cloth diaper safe."

## Recommendation (Considering Our Situation)

*Added 2026-02-28*

**For apartment living without confirmed in-unit laundry, disposable diapers are the practical default.** Here is the suggested approach:

1. **Start disposable** with Pampers Swaddlers or Huggies Little Snugglers — buy small packs of 2-3 brands before baby arrives to try once born
2. **Don't overbuy newborn size** — get 1-2 packs max; most babies move to Size 1 within 2-4 weeks (some skip NB entirely if born 8+ lbs, and July babies in warm weather may run smaller in diapers)
3. **Set up Subscribe & Save** on Amazon once you know which brand works — saves 5-15% automatically
4. **Get the Ubbi pail** — the ongoing savings on bags pays for itself within a year, and the steel + slim design works well in small spaces
5. **If you do have in-unit laundry** and are interested in cloth, consider a hybrid approach: cloth at home, disposable for daycare/outings. Start with a small trial stash (6-8 pocket diapers from Mama Koala, ~$50) before committing to a full set

> **Discuss with partner:** Main decision points are (1) cloth vs disposable (depends on laundry situation), (2) premium vs budget diaper brand (try a few and see what fits), and (3) subscription vs buy-as-needed. None of these are permanent — you can switch anytime.
