# Nice-to-Have Items

> Items that are helpful but not essential. Good registry additions. Don't buy until you see what you get as gifts and what baby actually likes.

## First 3 Months

| Item | Why | Priority | Notes |
|------|-----|----------|-------|
| Bouncer / rocker | Somewhere safe to set baby down | **Ready** | **Gifted:** BabyBjorn Bouncer |
| Swing | Soothes some babies | Low | Takes up space; try bouncer first |
| Play mat / activity gym | Tummy time, stimulation | Medium | Useful from ~1 month; Lovevery is popular but pricey |
| Portable sound machine | For outings, naps on the go | Medium | Yogasleep Hushh 2 or similar |
| Diaper bag / backpack | Organized outings | Medium | A regular backpack works fine too |
| Baby bathtub / bath flower | Easier than sink | Low | Skip if sink works for you |
| Night light (dimmable) | Night feeds without waking everyone | Medium | Hatch Rest does double duty as sound machine |

## 3–6 Months

| Item | Why | Notes |
|------|-----|-------|
| Exersaucer / jumper | Contained entertainment once baby can hold head up | Big footprint — skip if space is tight |
| High chair | Starting solids ~6 months | See [high-chair.md](high-chair.md) — Abiie Beyond recommended, IKEA Antilop as backup |
| Teething toys | Some babies teethe early | |
| Board books | Start reading early | Great gift/registry item |

## Nice but Not Necessary

- Baby Brezza (formula machine) — convenient but expensive
- Owlet Dream Sock or similar monitor sock — see [full analysis below](#wearable-pulse-oximeter-owlet-dream-sock)
- Fancy nursery decor — baby doesn't care
- Baby shoes — not until walking

### Wearable Pulse Oximeter (Owlet Dream Sock)

The Owlet Dream Sock is a wearable infant pulse oximeter that tracks heart rate and blood oxygen (SpO2) while baby sleeps. It's the most-asked-about "peace of mind" product in baby gear — and one where the evidence and the marketing tell very different stories.

#### What It Is

| Spec | Detail |
|------|--------|
| **Product** | Owlet Dream Sock |
| **Price** | ~$300 (Verified: [Owlet](https://owletcare.com/products/owlet-dream-sock), 2026-03-15) |
| **Subscription** | Not required for core features (live readings, alerts). Optional Owlet360 at $9.99/mo for trend data |
| **FDA Status** | De Novo clearance, Nov 2023 — first and only OTC infant pulse oximeter (Source: [FDA](https://www.accessdata.fda.gov/cdrh_docs/pdf22/DEN220091.pdf)) |
| **Cleared for** | Displaying pulse rate and oxygen level. **Not cleared for SIDS prevention or diagnosis** |
| **Accuracy** | ±3% SpO2 vs arterial blood gas; tested across all skin tones (Fitzpatrick I–VI) |
| **Age range** | 1–18 months, 6–30 lbs |
| **Colors** | Mint, Bedtime Blue, Dusty Rose |
| **HSA/FSA** | Eligible |

#### What the Evidence Says

**Does pulse oximetry monitoring reduce SIDS risk?**

*Evidence:* **No.** No RCT or systematic review has demonstrated that home cardiorespiratory monitoring (pulse oximetry or otherwise) reduces SIDS deaths in healthy infants. This is the consistent finding across multiple reviews. (Source: [Systematic review, PMC 2022](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9227273/))

**What does the AAP say?**

*Evidence:* The AAP 2022 safe sleep guidelines explicitly state: "Do not use home cardiorespiratory monitors as a strategy to reduce the risk of SIDS." They further warn that "the use of products claiming to increase sleep safety may provide a false sense of security and complacency for caregivers." (Source: [AAP 2022 Safe Sleep Guidelines](https://publications.aap.org/pediatrics/article/150/1/e2022057990/188304))

**What about the FDA clearance?**

The FDA cleared the Dream Sock as a *pulse oximeter* — a device that displays heart rate and oxygen levels. This means the device accurately measures what it claims to measure (±3%). It does **not** mean the FDA found it prevents SIDS or improves infant outcomes. Owlet's own labeling states: "Not intended to diagnose, treat or cure any disease or other condition including SIDS." (Source: [Owlet FAQ](https://owletcare.com/blogs/all/frequently-asked-questions-on-fda-clearance-and-why-it-matters-for-your-baby))

**How accurate is it?**

The FDA-cleared Dream Sock is a significant improvement over the original Smart Sock (which was pulled from market in 2021 after an FDA warning letter). The original had 88.8% sensitivity / 85.7% specificity for hypoxemia, and **0% sensitivity for bradycardia** — it missed every bradycardia event. (Source: [PMC 2021 study](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7849797/)) The new Dream Sock went through rigorous FDA testing and meets medical-grade pulse oximetry standards, so accuracy has materially improved.

**Does it cause parental anxiety?**

*Evidence is mixed.* Owlet claims 96% of owners report "more peace of mind" — but this is a manufacturer survey, not independent research. Independent research on home pulse oximetry finds false alarms cause "anxiety and psychological stress in parents" and can lead to "emotional stress, frustration and depression." Parents of infants prescribed medical monitors report significant sleep disruption from false alarms. (Source: [PMC 2022 systematic review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9227273/))

**Anecdotal benefit: catching illness**

*Anecdotal:* Parent testimonials describe the Dream Sock detecting low oxygen during RSV infections, prompting ER visits. These are real stories but not controlled evidence — RSV typically presents with visible symptoms (labored breathing, retractions, poor feeding) that parents can observe without a device. The Dream Sock may catch desaturation slightly earlier than symptom observation in some cases, but no study has quantified this benefit or shown it changes outcomes.

#### Oster Framework Analysis

| Factor | Assessment |
|--------|------------|
| **Baseline risk** | SIDS: ~0.33 per 1,000 live births (~1 in 3,000). SUID overall: ~1 per 1,000. Already low |
| **Evidence-supported interventions** | Back sleeping, firm surface, room sharing 0–6 months, pacifier — these are free and collectively reduce risk substantially |
| **Does this device reduce SIDS?** | No evidence it does. No RCT, no observational study, no systematic review supports this |
| **What it actually does** | Accurately reads SpO2 and pulse rate. Alerts when readings leave preset ranges |
| **Cost of mitigation** | $300 + potential false alarm anxiety + potential false sense of security. May reduce vigilance about interventions that actually work |
| **What would Oster say?** | The baseline SIDS risk is already very low. The interventions proven to reduce it are free. This $300 device has no demonstrated effect on the number it's marketed against. If it genuinely gives you peace of mind, that has personal value — but that's a comfort purchase, not a safety purchase |

> **Bottom line:** The Dream Sock is an accurate pulse oximeter. It is not a SIDS prevention device. No consumer monitor has been shown to reduce SIDS risk. If you follow the evidence-based safe sleep practices (back sleeping, firm surface, room sharing, pacifier), you're already doing the things that actually move the needle. The Dream Sock's real value proposition is parental peace of mind — which is legitimate but subjective, and for some parents backfires into increased anxiety.
