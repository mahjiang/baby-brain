# Crib / Bassinet

> **Status:** Decided | **Buy by:** Week 28-30 (bassinet), Week 32-34 (crib) | **Buy new:** YES (safety-critical)

## What to Know

- AAP recommends **room-sharing** (not bed-sharing) for at least 6 months, ideally 12 months (Source: [AAP Safe Sleep](https://publications.aap.org/pediatrics/article/150/1/e2022057990/188304))
- Sleep surface must be **firm, flat, and bare** — no pillows, bumpers, blankets, or toys
- Must meet current CPSC safety standards
- For small spaces, a bassinet or mini crib is often the right first choice
- **No weighted swaddles or sleep sacks** — AAP strongly advises against as of 2024 update; added weight can restrict chest expansion and breathing (Source: [AAP Safe Sleep](https://www.aap.org/en/patient-care/safe-sleep/))
- **"Bare is Best"** — CPSC and AAP agree: nothing in the crib/bassinet except a firm mattress with a fitted sheet (Source: [CPSC Safe Sleep](https://www.cpsc.gov/SafeSleep))
- **Crib bumpers are banned** — federal crib bumper ban is in effect; they can obstruct infant breathing (Source: [CPSC 2025 Warnings](https://www.cpsc.gov/SafeSleep))
- **Buy only CPSC-compliant products from established brands** — CPSC has issued multiple 2024-2025 warnings against off-brand convertible cribs, aftermarket mattresses, and baby loungers sold on Amazon that fail federal safety standards

## Research & Reviews

### AAP Safe Sleep Guidelines — Current Evidence

*Added 2026-02-28*

The AAP's most recent major safe sleep policy update was published in June 2022 (reaffirmed through 2024-2025) with these key recommendations:

- **Room-sharing without bed-sharing** for at least 6 months (reduces SIDS risk by up to 50%)
- **Supine (back) sleep position** for every sleep until age 1
- **Firm, flat surface** — crib, bassinet, or portable play yard that meets CPSC standards
- **Nothing in the sleep space** except fitted sheet ("Bare is Best")
- **No inclined sleepers, baby loungers, or dock-a-tots for sleep** — these do not meet safe sleep standards
- **No bed-sharing** — risk of sleep-related death is 5-10x higher when baby is under 4 months
- Additional protective factors: breastfeeding, pacifier use at sleep, up-to-date immunizations

(Source: [AAP Policy — Sleep-Related Infant Deaths](https://publications.aap.org/pediatrics/article/150/1/e2022057990/188304))
(Source: [HealthyChildren.org Safe Sleep Guide](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/sleep/Pages/a-parents-guide-to-safe-sleep.aspx))

> **Key takeaway:** Any CPSC-compliant bassinet, mini crib, or full crib with a firm flat mattress and fitted sheet is equally safe. The choice between them is about space, convenience, and lifespan — not safety.

### Bassinet vs. Mini Crib vs. Pack 'n Play — Which to Buy?

*Added 2026-02-28*

**The case for starting with a bassinet or bedside sleeper (Phase 1):**
- AAP recommends room-sharing; a bassinet fits next to the bed where a crib often won't
- Smallest footprint matters most in the first months when you're in the bedroom together
- Night feeds are easier with a bedside sleeper — fold-down side means less getting up
- Bassinets typically last birth to ~5 months (or until baby rolls/pushes up/hits ~20 lbs)

**The case for a mini crib (Phase 2 or skip-ahead option):**
- Longer usable life (birth to 18-24 months) means better cost-per-use
- Many convert to toddler bed or twin bed — extends value further
- If your bedroom can fit a mini crib (need ~25"x40" of floor space), you could skip the bassinet entirely
- Standard mini crib mattress size (24"x38") is widely available

**Pack 'n Play as a versatile alternative:**
- Works as bassinet (with newborn insert), crib, and play yard
- Portable between rooms and for travel
- Mattress is thinner and less comfortable than a dedicated crib mattress — fine for sleep per CPSC, but some parents report baby sleeps better on a firmer dedicated mattress
- Good value for the multi-use, but not a great permanent sleep solution past ~12 months

(Source: [GoodBuyGear — Pack 'n Play vs. Crib](https://goodbuygear.com/blogs/gear-guidance/pack-n-play-vs-crib))
(Source: [WonderBaby — Bassinet vs Pack 'n Play](https://www.wonderbaby.org/articles/bassinet-vs-pack-n-play))

### Cat Safety Solutions

*Added 2026-02-28*

**Why it matters:** Cats are heat-seeking and attracted to cozy enclosed spaces. A newborn cannot turn their head or push a cat away. The risk is suffocation if a cat curls up against or on a baby's face. This is not a myth — veterinarians and pediatricians both flag it as a genuine concern. (Source: [ASPCA — Cats and Babies](https://www.aspca.org/pet-care/cat-care/cats-and-babies))

**Strategies ranked by effectiveness:**

1. **Closed door / physical separation** (most effective)
   - Keep bedroom door closed during naps if baby is in a separate room
   - In shared-room setup (which you'll have), this isn't always practical
   - A screen door on the bedroom can let you hear baby while keeping cat out

2. **Breathable mesh crib/bassinet covers or tents** (effective physical barrier)
   - Pop-up crib tents with mesh tops and zippered access (e.g., Pro Baby Safety Pop-Up Crib Tent, ~$30-50)
   - Must be breathable mesh — never use non-breathable covers
   - **CPSC caution:** In 2023, CPSC warned against one brand (CCATTO) of baby tent for suffocation risk. Stick with established brands and check for current recalls before buying. (Source: [CPSC CCATTO Warning](https://www.cpsc.gov/Warnings/2023/CPSC-Warns-Consumers-to-Immediately-Stop-Using-CCATTO-Baby-Tents-Due-to-Suffocation-Risk-and-Fall-Hazard-Failure-to-Meet-Infant-Sleep-Product-Safety-Requirements))
   - Many bassinets with full mesh sides and canopy/cover (like the Chicco Close to You) make cat access harder by design

3. **Bassinets with built-in canopy or hood** (moderate deterrent)
   - Some bassinets come with a retractable canopy that covers part or all of the opening
   - Not fully cat-proof on their own, but combined with other deterrents they help
   - The HALO BassiNest's mesh walls and lowering side wall design make it harder (but not impossible) for cats to settle in

4. **Deterrents on/around the sleep surface**
   - Double-sided tape on edges (cats dislike sticky surfaces)
   - Aluminum foil around the bassinet rim (cats dislike the texture and sound)
   - Citrus-scented sprays near (not in) the sleep area (cats dislike citrus)
   - *Anecdotal:* Many parents report success with starting deterrents weeks before baby arrives so the cat learns to avoid the sleep area

5. **Training and behavior modification**
   - Set up crib/bassinet early and consistently redirect cat away from it
   - Use a squirt bottle when cat approaches the sleep surface (cat should not see you do it — associates the crib itself with unpleasantness)
   - Provide the cat with alternative cozy sleeping spots nearby

> **Our situation:** With one cat in an apartment where the bassinet will be in the bedroom, a combination approach is best: (1) choose a bassinet/sleeper with full mesh sides that make it physically awkward for the cat, (2) add a breathable mesh cover/tent from a reputable brand, and (3) start deterrent training 4-6 weeks before due date. See [cat prep](../03-space/cat-prep.md) for full plan.

(Source: [Catster — 9 Ways to Keep Cats Out of Crib](https://www.catster.com/lifestyle/keep-cats-out-of-crib/))
(Source: [WonderBaby — Cat Cover for Bassinet](https://www.wonderbaby.org/articles/cat-cover-for-bassinet))
(Source: [GetForBaby — How to Keep Cat Out of Bassinet](https://getforbaby.com/how-to-keep-cat-out-of-bassinet/))
