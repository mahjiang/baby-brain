## Context

Baby needs a safe, supportive seat for starting solids around 6 months. This is a daily-use item for 2+ years minimum, so it's worth getting right. Key question: spend $25 on the internet's favorite budget chair, or invest in a grow-with-child option that fits the buy-once philosophy?

---

## The Footrest Question

*Evidence:* Pediatric feeding therapists (SLPs and OTs) consistently recommend the **90-90-90 position** for infant feeding: 90 degrees at hips, 90 degrees at knees, 90 degrees at ankles with feet flat on a surface. This is not just ergonomic preference — proper foot support provides core stability, which improves oral motor control for chewing and swallowing and reduces choking risk. (Source: [Solid Starts — Ultimate Guide to High Chairs](https://solidstarts.com/the-ultimate-guide-to-high-chairs-for-babies/), [Kids Eat in Color — High Chair Footrests](https://kidseatincolor.com/high-chair-with-foot-rest/))

Without a footrest, dangling feet shift the center of gravity backward, reducing control of the muscles needed for chewing and swallowing. This can lead to shorter mealtimes, squirming, distracted eating, and poor oral motor control. (Source: [Creative Speech Therapy NYC](https://www.cst-nyc.com/the-importance-of-picking-the-right-high-chair/))

> **Takeaway:** An adjustable footrest is close to non-negotiable for a primary high chair. Any option without one needs an aftermarket footrest add-on.

## New vs Used

High chairs are in the **"used is fine"** category — unlike car seats (which expire and can have hidden crash damage) or cribs (which have strict evolving safety standards), a high chair's safety is visually inspectable. There's no expiration date, no hidden structural history to worry about, and failure modes (cracked wood, missing hardware) are obvious on inspection.

> **Takeaway:** A used Stokke Tripp Trapp at ~$100–150 gets you the "dream" grow-with-child chair at Abiie-level pricing. This is a compelling option for buy-once families on a moderate budget.

### Used High Chair Safety Checklist

Before buying any used high chair, verify all six:

1. **CPSC recall check** — Search the model at [cpsc.gov/Recalls](https://www.cpsc.gov/Recalls) or call 1-800-638-2772. Example: the Bugaboo Giraffe was recalled in 2024 for a leg-separation fall hazard — this is exactly the kind of thing that isn't obvious from photos alone. (Source: [CPSC — Bugaboo Giraffe Recall](https://www.cpsc.gov/Recalls/2025/Bugaboo-Recalls-Giraffe-High-Chairs-Due-to-Fall-Hazard))
2. **Post-June 2019 manufacture** — ASTM F404-20 tightened stability and restraint standards. Look for a date stamp on the frame or check the serial number with the manufacturer. Chairs made before this date may not meet current safety requirements.
3. **Harness intact** — All straps present, stitching solid, buckle clicks firmly. A 3-point or 5-point harness should have zero fraying or wear.
4. **All hardware present and tight** — Every bolt, screw, and connector accounted for. Wobble-test the assembled chair. No stripped threads.
5. **Structural stability** — No cracks, splits, or warping in wood/plastic. Apply moderate pressure from multiple angles — it should feel rock-solid.
6. **Complete set** — Confirm which accessories are included (Baby Set, tray, harness, rail). Missing parts can cost $30–70 each to replace, eroding the savings.

## Our Situation

- **Apartment, no nursery:** Floor space is premium. Chairs that push under the dining table (Stokke, Abiie) have a major advantage over the IKEA Antilop's splayed legs.
- **2 cats:** Easy cleaning matters — cats will investigate any food residue. The Abiie's waterproof cushion and the Antilop's hose-off simplicity both score well here. Stokke's wood is fine but the optional fabric cushion needs more attention.
- **Buy-once preference:** The Stokke and Abiie both serve as a dining chair through adulthood. The IKEA is disposable by age 3 — you'll buy a booster seat next, then a regular chair. Total lifetime cost may be similar.
- **Moderate budget:** The Abiie at ~$190 hits the sweet spot — grow-with-child like the Stokke but ~$150 less, and it includes the tray and cushion that Stokke charges extra for. A used Stokke Tripp Trapp at ~$100–150 is even better value if you find one in good condition — the same chair that retails for $350+ at a price below the Abiie.
- **Timeline:** Not needed until ~6 months (December 2026). Good registry candidate at any price point. Plenty of time to watch for a used Stokke deal.

## Recommendation

> **Best value: Used Stokke Tripp Trapp (~$100–150)** if you find one in good condition. This gets you the gold-standard grow-with-child chair — 50-year pedigree, 300 lb capacity, pushes under the table — at a price *below* the Abiie. >
> **Best new option: Abiie Beyond Junior Y (~$190) because:**
>
> 1. It's the best value *new* grow-with-child chair — same European beechwood and adjustable footrest as the Stokke, but $150+ cheaper and includes the tray and waterproof cushion
> 2. BabyGearLab's #1 rated high chair for ease of use and cleaning
> 3. Pushes under the table — critical for apartment life
> 4. Tool-free seat adjustment makes it easy to reposition as baby grows
> 5. Aligns with buy-once philosophy (250 lb capacity, lasts to adulthood)
>
> **Registry dream item:** Stokke Tripp Trapp new (~$350+) if the used route doesn't pan out and budget allows. The 50-year pedigree and slightly more adjustability justify the premium if someone else is buying.
>
> **Keep an IKEA Antilop as a $25 backup** for grandparents' house, travel, or messy outdoor eating. Don't rely on it as the primary chair — the lack of footrest and space footprint are real drawbacks in an apartment.
