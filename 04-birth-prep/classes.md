# Classes

## Our Situation

- First-time parents — higher benefit from classes (evidence strongest for primiparous women)
- Pregnancy complications being monitored — makes childbirth education more valuable (understanding interventions, flexibility)
- Urban/suburban — good access to both hospital-based and independent class providers
- Both partners get paid leave — both can attend classes together
- Moderate budget — factor in class costs ($0-$300+ depending on type)
- Evidence-based approach — want data, not ideology, about birth options
- Due ~July 2026 — target completing all classes by week 36 (late May/early June)

## Research: What Types of Classes Are Worth It?

### The Evidence on Childbirth Education

*Evidence:* A systematic review of primiparous women found prenatal education was effective at reducing fear and anxiety about childbirth and enhancing self-efficacy. Clinical outcomes were more modest: women who took classes had lower induction rates (17% vs 30%) and lower analgesic use (29% vs 54%), but cesarean rates were comparable. (Source: [PMC/Effects of Childbirth Education on Maternity Outcomes](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6984379/); [PMC/Influence of Antenatal Education on Birth Outcomes](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11320171/))

*Evidence quality:* Moderate — multiple studies with consistent direction, but significant heterogeneity in study design, small sample sizes, and short follow-up periods. The Cochrane review on antenatal education (Gagnon, 2007) concluded the effects "remain largely unknown" due to lack of high-quality RCTs. (Source: [Cochrane/Individual or group antenatal education](https://www.cochranelibrary.com/cdsr/doi/10.1002/14651858.CD002869.pub2/full))

*What Oster would say:* The biggest measurable benefit of childbirth classes is psychological — reduced anxiety, increased confidence, and better preparation for decision-making during labor. The clinical outcome data (lower inductions, less analgesic use) is encouraging but not definitive. For a first-time parent who is evidence-based and currently feeling overwhelmed, the anxiety-reduction benefit alone is probably worth the cost. The key is choosing a class that teaches informed decision-making rather than pushing a specific ideology.

### Partner Training: The Strongest Evidence

*Evidence:* An RCT found that when a lay support person was taught doula-style supportive techniques in just two 2-hour sessions, birthing mothers experienced shorter labor, delayed epidural use, higher Apgar scores at 1 and 5 minutes, and increased satisfaction. A follow-up found these mothers also reported more positive perceptions of their infants, increased self-worth, and higher breastfeeding rates. (Source: [PubMed/Randomized control trial of continuous support by lay doula](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16881989/); [PMC/Impact of Doulas on Healthy Birth Outcomes](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3647727/))

*Evidence quality:* Moderate-strong (RCT with follow-up). This is some of the best evidence in the childbirth education space.

> **Key takeaway:** Partner-focused training in hands-on support techniques (counter-pressure, massage, hip squeeze, positioning, breathing coaching) has better evidence than any specific childbirth philosophy. Emily Oster emphasizes this point — training your partner as your support person may be the highest-ROI class investment. (Source: [ParentData/Experiencing Labor and Delivery](https://parentdata.org/experiencing-labor-and-delivery/))

## Class-by-Class Breakdown

### 1. Childbirth / Labor Prep — HIGH PRIORITY

**What it covers:** Stages of labor, coping techniques, pain management options, when to go to the hospital, interventions (induction, epidural, C-section), partner support role, decision-making frameworks.

#### Types of Childbirth Classes

| Type | Philosophy | Duration | Partner Focus | Evidence Base | Situation Fit |
|------|-----------|----------|---------------|---------------|---------------|
| **Hospital-based general** | Pragmatic; covers your specific hospital's policies, pain management options, when to come in | 1-day or 2-4 evenings | Moderate | No RCTs comparing class types. Practical value: you learn *your* hospital's specific procedures | **Excellent** — learn your specific hospital's protocols; often free or low-cost; relevant given complications |
| **Independent comprehensive (e.g., Evidence Based Birth, Birthsmarter)** | Evidence-based, unbiased; covers advocacy, BRAIN framework (Benefits, Risks, Alternatives, Instincts, Nothing) | 4-8+ hours online or multi-week in-person | Strong | Teaching decision-making frameworks aligns with best evidence on patient autonomy | **Excellent** — matches evidence-based approach; good complement to hospital class |
| **Lamaze** | Originally breathing-focused, now broader evidence-based approach emphasizing informed decision-making and individual preferences | 6-12 hours over multiple sessions | Moderate | Oldest method, widely studied but no RCTs showing superiority over other formats | **Good** — evidence-based evolution, but may overlap with a comprehensive independent class |
| **Bradley Method** | Unmedicated birth focus; heavy partner involvement as "coach"; nutrition, exercise, relaxation | 12 weeks (!), ~24 hours total | Very strong — partner is central | Low-quality evidence (self-reported data from the organization). Reported cesarean rates 10-25% vs national 32%, but selection bias is likely | **Fair** — best partner training, but 12-week commitment is heavy; strong unmedicated philosophy may not fit if complications require flexibility |
| **HypnoBirthing** | Self-hypnosis, deep relaxation, reframing pain; positive visualization | 5 sessions over 5 weeks | Moderate | Reported lower epidural use (20% vs 76% national) and 17% cesarean rate, but data is from the company itself with no external review. Cochrane: hypnosis in labor shows "less pain, decreased time in active labor" but evidence quality is low | **Fair** — interesting techniques, but data quality is poor; philosophy may conflict with need for flexibility given complications |

(Source: [PMC/Comparison of Bradley and HypnoBirthing](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4744344/))

*Evidence quality on class type comparisons:* Very weak. No well-designed RCTs compare these methods head-to-head. The one published comparison (Varner, 2015) found "the paucity of evidence does not support provider recommendation of one method over the other." Self-reported outcome data from class organizations is unreliable due to selection bias and no external review.

> **Our situation:** Given pregnancy complications and an evidence-based mindset, a hospital class + an independent evidence-based class is the strongest combination. The hospital class teaches you *their* specific protocols (critical when complications may change plans). An independent class adds unbiased decision-making frameworks and deeper partner training. Skip the 12-week Bradley commitment — you can get the partner-training benefit from a shorter format.

> **For partner discussion:** Do we want to do just the hospital class (probably free, covers the basics), or add an independent evidence-based class ($100-$250) for deeper preparation and advocacy skills?

#### Timing
Sign up now (week 21-22). Take the class between weeks 24-32. Earlier is better — gives time to practice techniques and digest information before the third trimester gets physically demanding.

### 2. Infant CPR / First Aid — HIGH PRIORITY

**What it covers:** Infant choking response, infant CPR technique, basic first aid.

*Evidence:* Bystander CPR doubles survival from out-of-hospital cardiac arrest. For parents specifically, trained parents showed knowledge scores jumping from 47% to 94% immediately after training. Six infants survived with good neurologic status following CPR events performed by parents trained via self-instructional kits. (Source: [RQI Partners/Importance of CPR Skills for Parents](https://rqipartners.com/resources/blog/the-importance-of-cpr-skills-for-parents/); [PubMed/Self-instructional CPR training for parents](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22353642/))

*Evidence quality:* Strong for bystander CPR improving survival (meta-analyses). Moderate for parent-specific training (smaller studies, but consistent direction).

**Critical finding on skill retention:** CPR skills decay fast. Only ~33% of trained parents could demonstrate adequate skills after 6 months. The Red Cross Scientific Advisory Council confirms less than half of participants pass a skills test one year after training. (Source: [PubMed/Retention and use of CPR skills in parents](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9987420/); [Red Cross](https://www.redcross.org/take-a-class/cpr))

*What Oster would say:* Infant cardiac arrest is rare in healthy infants, but when it happens, bystander response is the single biggest determinant of outcome. The cost of a 2-4 hour class is low relative to the potential benefit. The real question isn't "should we take a class" (yes) — it's how to maintain the skill. Plan on a refresher at 6-12 months postpartum.

| Format | Cost | Duration | Hands-On Practice | Skill Retention | Situation Fit |
|--------|------|----------|-------------------|-----------------|---------------|
| **In-person (Red Cross / AHA / hospital)** | $25-$80 | 2-4 hours | Yes — manikin practice with instructor feedback; 2:1 student-to-manikin ratio in updated Red Cross program | Best retention; instructor corrects technique in real-time | **Excellent** — hands-on practice is essential for a physical skill; worth the scheduling effort |
| **Blended (online learning + in-person skills session)** | $30-$60 | Online module + 1-2 hour skills session | Yes — reduced in-person time, more efficient | Good — still get hands-on correction | **Good** — good compromise if scheduling is tight |
| **Online-only (video)** | $0-$30 | 1-2 hours | No — watch only | Poorest; video-prepared students performed better than unprepared, but hands-on practice is where real learning happens | **Poor for primary training** — acceptable only as supplement or refresher, not as your only CPR training |

(Source: [PMC/Use of instructional video to prepare parents for learning infant CPR](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2666859/); [PMC/Low-Dose High-Frequency CPR Training](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3387915/))

> **Key takeaway:** Take an in-person CPR class. This is a physical skill — you need to feel the correct compression depth and rhythm on a manikin. Video can supplement but not replace hands-on practice. Plan a refresher ~6 months after the baby arrives, when skills have decayed but the choking risk is increasing (solid food introduction).

> **Our situation:** Both parents and any regular caregivers (grandparents) should take this class. The AAP recommends that everyone who cares for an infant learn CPR. (Source: [HealthyChildren.org/AHA AAP CPR Guidelines](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/news/Pages/AHA-and-AAP-release-update-CPR-guidelines-to-help-save-young-lives.aspx))

### 3. Breastfeeding / Newborn Feeding — MEDIUM PRIORITY

**What it covers:** Latch technique, positioning, milk supply basics, common problems (engorgement, mastitis), pumping introduction, when to supplement, formula basics.

*Evidence:* A Cochrane review found no conclusive evidence that prenatal breastfeeding education improves breastfeeding duration or initiation rates. A 2021 systematic review found educational interventions effective at increasing breastfeeding duration up to 2 months postpartum, but not beyond. Prenatal education does improve maternal knowledge. (Source: [PMC/Antenatal breastfeeding education Cochrane](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6463791/); [PMC/Effects of Prenatal Breastfeeding Education](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11420594/))

*What Oster would say:* The evidence for prenatal breastfeeding classes improving actual outcomes is surprisingly weak. The biggest predictors of breastfeeding success are postpartum support (lactation consultant, partner support) and institutional practices (baby-friendly hospital, skin-to-skin, avoiding unnecessary supplementation). Your prenatal class dollar might be better spent reserving a postpartum lactation consultant visit. That said, knowing the basics beforehand means you're not learning everything for the first time while sleep-deprived with a crying newborn.

> **Our situation:** Worth taking, especially because you're first-time parents, but temper expectations — the real learning happens postpartum. A hospital-based breastfeeding class is usually free or low-cost and sufficient for prenatal preparation. Budget more for postpartum lactation support. See [breastfeeding prep](../06-feeding/breastfeeding-prep.md) for the full plan.

### 4. Newborn Care Basics — MEDIUM-LOW PRIORITY

**What it covers:** Bathing, diapering, swaddling, soothing techniques, umbilical cord care, what's normal vs what needs a doctor, safe sleep setup.

*Evidence:* Limited formal evidence on class effectiveness. An NIH study found mothers who took classes reported feeling more prepared and more confident. Hospital nurses cover these basics before discharge, so there is a safety net. (Source: [Office on Women's Health/Birthing, breastfeeding, and parenting classes](https://womenshealth.gov/pregnancy/getting-ready-baby/birthing-breast-and-parenting-classes))

*What Oster would say:* These skills are straightforward and learnable on the fly. Humans have been figuring out diapering and bathing without classes for millennia. The value is psychological — reducing that "I've never held a baby" anxiety for first-time parents. If you're already taking a childbirth class and a CPR class, this is lower priority. Many childbirth classes include a newborn care module.

> **Our situation:** Nice to have but not essential as a standalone class, especially if your childbirth class includes a newborn care module (many do). Hospital nurses will also teach the basics before discharge. If you're feeling overwhelmed, skip the separate class and learn these skills in the hospital.

### 5. Car Seat Safety — LOW PRIORITY (but do the free inspection)

**What it covers:** Proper installation, harness adjustment, common mistakes.

*Evidence:* NHTSA estimates up to 59% of car seats are installed incorrectly. The marginal safety difference between car seat models is small compared to the difference between correct and incorrect installation. (Source: [NHTSA/Car Seats and Booster Seats](https://www.nhtsa.gov/equipment/car-seats-and-booster-seats))

> **Our situation:** You don't need a class — you need a free CPST (Certified Child Passenger Safety Technician) inspection after you install the car seat. This is the highest-ROI safety intervention for car seats. Safe Kids hosts 8,000+ free events nationwide. Find one at [cert.safekids.org/get-car-seat-checked](https://cert.safekids.org/get-car-seat-checked) or [NHTSA's inspection station locator](https://www.nhtsa.gov/equipment/car-seats-and-booster-seats). See [car seat](../02-gear/car-seat.md) for the full decision.

## Online vs In-Person: What the Evidence Says

*Evidence:* Direct comparison research is limited. The available evidence suggests:

| Factor | In-Person | Online | Evidence Quality |
|--------|-----------|--------|-----------------|
| **Anxiety reduction** | Effective | Also effective — one RCT (n=90) found an interactive online platform significantly reduced third-trimester anxiety vs usual care (44.6 vs 53.9, p<0.01) | Moderate (single RCT, small sample) |
| **Satisfaction** | High | High when well-designed — same RCT found 95% vs 65% satisfaction | Moderate |
| **Physical skills (CPR, breastfeeding latch)** | Superior — hands-on practice with instructor feedback | Inferior — can't practice compression depth on a video | Strong consensus, limited formal comparison |
| **Partner engagement** | Both attend together in same room | Flexibility helps busy schedules; risk of passive watching | No comparative data |
| **Peer support / community** | Stronger — meet other local expecting parents | Weaker — no social connection | Observational only |
| **Scheduling flexibility** | Fixed times, may conflict with work | Watch anytime, rewatch later | N/A |
| **Hospital-specific info** | Yes — learn your hospital's protocols, tour the unit | No — generic content | N/A |
| **Cost** | $0-$300+ | $0-$200 | N/A |

(Source: [PMC/Interactive childbirth education platform RCT](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10330277/); [PMC/Importance of online childbirth preparation courses](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11002958/))

*Evidence quality on online vs in-person:* Low. Researchers have called for "high-quality, randomized trials with sufficient sample sizes" to properly compare formats. Most existing evidence is from COVID-era adaptations, not designed comparisons.

> **Bottom line:** The format matters less than whether you actually do it. That said, here's the practical split:
> - **In-person is better for:** CPR (hands-on skill), hospital tour/orientation, meeting other parents
> - **Online is better for:** Flexibility, rewatchability, deeper content, supplementing a short hospital class
> - **Best approach for most first-time parents:** Hospital in-person class (protocols + tour) + quality online course (deeper education + advocacy tools). This is what Emily Oster's advice converges on — use the hospital class for logistics and an independent course for real preparation.
