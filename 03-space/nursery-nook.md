# Nursery Nook -- Small-Space Plan

> **Status:** Ready to discuss | Anatomy scan clear (week 21). No dedicated nursery room -- designing a functional baby zone within our shared bedroom.

## Our Constraints

- Apartment (no spare bedroom for nursery)
- 2 cats in the household (needs physical barriers around sleep space)
- Car + transit use (gear storage matters)
- Moderate budget
- July 2026 baby (summer heat in apartment -- airflow and temperature management are critical)
- Buy-once preference where practical

## Space Planning Framework

### Step 1: Measure and Map

- [ ] Measure the area designated for baby zone (L x W)
- [ ] Confirm full-size crib footprint (~54"x30") fits in bedroom -- the DaVinci Sammy Scallop is 53.75"x29.5"x35"H
- [ ] Note outlet locations (for monitor, sound machine, night light)
- [ ] Identify bassinet placement next to bed (Phase 1: birth to ~5 months)
- [ ] Identify where full crib will go (Phase 2: ~5 months onward)
- [ ] Identify where diaper changing will happen (dresser with changing pad)
- [ ] Identify feeding spot(s)
- [ ] Measure bed height to confirm bassinet compatibility (Arm's Reach Clear-Vue fits beds 24-30" -- already confirmed compatible per [crib-bassinet.md](../02-gear/crib-bassinet.md))

### Step 2: The Three Zones

A nursery nook needs just three functional areas:

**1. Sleep Zone**

*Phase 1 (birth to ~5 months):* Bassinet next to your bed per AAP room-sharing recommendation.

Our decided options (see [crib-bassinet.md](../02-gear/crib-bassinet.md)):
- **Arm's Reach Clear-Vue** (~$200) -- BabyGearLab's top-rated sleeping surface, all-mesh sides for summer airflow, wheels for room-to-room use. Fits beds 24-30" high (confirmed compatible). (Source: [BabyGearLab Bassinet Reviews](https://www.babygearlab.com/topics/nursery-furniture/best-bassinet))
- **HALO BassiNest 4.0** (~$280) -- 360-degree swivel, base slides under bed to minimize footprint, height adjustable up to 30", mesh walls. Available March 2026 with redesigned lower-profile legs. Baby Safety Alliance certified. (Source: [HALO BassiNest 4.0 Launch](https://www.prweb.com/releases/halo-launches-bassinest-swivel-sleeper-4-0--reinforcing-the-standard-in-safer-closer-sleep-302684878.html))

*Phase 2 (~5 months onward):* Full-size convertible crib in bedroom or other room area.
- **DaVinci Sammy Scallop 4-in-1** (~$299) -- 53.75"x29.5"x35"H, converts through to full-size bed. 4 mattress positions, 50 lb weight limit. GREENGUARD Gold, NZ pine. 4.8/5 rating. (Source: [DaVinci Baby](https://davincibaby.com/products/sammy-scallop-4-in-1-convertible-crib))
- **Babyletto Pure Core Mattress** (~$150-200) -- best breathability in-budget for July baby; dual firmness for convertible crib path; removable waterproof cover (practical with 2 cats)

Cat deterrent for sleep space is essential -- breathable mesh cover/tent (~$30-40) + closed bedroom door or deterrent training. See [cat prep](cat-prep.md).

**2. Changing Zone**
- A changing pad on top of a dresser (dual-purpose furniture -- dresser stores baby clothes, top serves as changing surface)
- Wall-mounted shelf or caddy above for supplies -- mount into studs, lightweight items only above where baby lies (see [baby-proofing.md](baby-proofing.md) for wall-mounting safety rules)
- No dedicated changing table needed -- saves significant space
- Keep diapers, wipes, cream, and change of clothes within arm's reach
- Portable diaper caddy for anywhere-in-apartment changes -- see [organization.md](organization.md) for caddy picks
- **Anchor dresser to wall** with anti-tip straps (TipNot Adhesive or Qdos Zero-Screw for renter-friendliness) -- CPSC data shows 459 child tip-over deaths 2000-2018, 64% involving dressers. This is a trivial-cost, one-time intervention that eliminates the risk entirely. See [baby-proofing.md](baby-proofing.md)

**3. Feeding Zone**
- A comfortable chair or your existing couch/bed
- Side table with water, snacks, phone charger, burp cloth
- Good lighting (dimmable for night feeds) -- a rechargeable touch-activated night light (~$10-20) provides low-level light for feeds without waking anyone fully
- Nursing pillow storage nearby

> **Glider note:** No clinical evidence that a glider produces better feeding outcomes than any comfortable chair. Test existing seating first -- a nursing pillow + footstool covers ~80% of the benefit at ~$30. If feeding sessions are uncomfortable after the first few weeks, a compact glider (~25" wide) is a reasonable later purchase. See [nice-to-have](../02-gear/nice-to-have.md#nursing-chair--glider).

### Step 3: Storage Solutions

See [organization.md](organization.md) for detailed storage ideas. Key principles for small spaces:

- **Go vertical** -- Wall shelves, over-door organizers, closet systems
- **Dual-purpose furniture** -- Dresser as changing table, ottoman with storage
- **Rotate, don't hoard** -- Keep only current-size clothes accessible; store ahead-sizes in lidded/zipped bins (essential with 2 cats -- see [organization.md](organization.md#cat-hair-management-2-cat-household))
- **Under-crib/bed storage** -- Flat bins (IKEA SKUBB or Iris Weathertight) for out-of-season items -- both keep cat hair out

## Research

### Room-Sharing: What the Evidence Actually Says

The AAP's 2022 updated policy statement (still current as of March 2026) recommends that infants sleep in the parents' room, on a separate firm surface, for **at least the first 6 months**. This is a notable shift from the 2016 guidelines, which recommended "ideally for the first year." The 2022 update quietly de-emphasized the 12-month target. (Source: [AAP 2022 Policy Statement -- Sleep-Related Infant Deaths](https://publications.aap.org/pediatrics/article/150/1/e2022057990/188304/Sleep-Related-Infant-Deaths-Updated-2022))

**The case for room-sharing (0-6 months):**
- *Evidence:* 90% of sudden unexpected infant deaths occur in the first 6 months. Case-control studies show infants sleeping in a separate room are 2.75 to 11.5 times more likely to die suddenly and unexpectedly compared to room-sharing infants. The AAP states room-sharing "can decrease the risk of SIDS by as much as 50%." (Source: [HealthyChildren.org -- How to Keep Your Sleeping Baby Safe](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/sleep/Pages/a-parents-guide-to-safe-sleep.aspx))
- *Evidence quality:* Moderate. Based on case-control studies (not RCTs), which are retrospective and subject to recall bias. However, the consistency across multiple studies and the magnitude of the effect (50% reduction) makes this a reasonable recommendation.

**The case against extended room-sharing (6-12 months):**
- *Evidence:* The INSIGHT study (Paul et al., 2017, *Pediatrics*) found that at 9 months, early independent sleepers (own room by 4 months) slept 40 minutes more per night than room-sharers. By 30 months, room-sharers at 9 months still slept 45 minutes less. Room-sharers were also 4x more likely to transition to bed-sharing overnight -- which the AAP strongly discourages. (Source: [Paul et al., INSIGHT Study, *Pediatrics* 2017](https://publications.aap.org/pediatrics/article-abstract/140/1/e20170122/37986/Mother-Infant-Room-Sharing-and-Sleep-Outcomes-in))
- *Evidence quality:* Moderate (prospective cohort, n=279, mostly white/higher-income sample -- may not generalize fully).

**Acknowledged tension in the literature:**
- *Evidence:* A 2024 commentary in *Pediatrics* directly addressed "The Tension Between AAP Safe Sleep Guidelines and Infant Sleep," noting that the room-sharing recommendation beyond 6 months relies on evidence that is "very sensitive to the assumptions made" and uses study designs with known limitations. The commentary signals growing professional acknowledgment that the 6-12 month recommendation is on weaker footing than the 0-6 month one. (Source: [Pediatrics 2024 -- The Tension Between AAP Safe Sleep Guidelines and Infant Sleep](https://publications.aap.org/pediatrics/article/153/4/e2023064675/196919/The-Tension-Between-AAP-Safe-Sleep-Guidelines-and))

**Emily Oster's analysis:**
- The evidence supporting room-sharing past 6 months is weak. The 2022 AAP update implicitly acknowledged this by shortening the recommendation. Oster notes "reasonable people will choose to room share for shorter periods or never room share at all." The strongest SIDS risk period is 0-4 months; by 6 months the absolute risk is very low. (Source: [ParentData -- Should I Put My Baby in Their Own Room After Four Months?](https://parentdata.org/qa-what-are-the-recommendations-for-room-sharing); [ParentData -- The Data Behind Safe Sleep](https://parentdata.org/back-sleeping-sids-new-research))

> **For partner discussion:** Room-sharing for the first 6 months is well-supported and aligns perfectly with our apartment setup (no spare bedroom). After 6 months, the evidence gets weaker and the sleep trade-offs get real. We can revisit at 6 months whether to transition to a crib in a separate sleeping area or continue room-sharing. No wrong answer here -- it depends on how everyone is sleeping.

For deeper safe sleep evidence, see [sleep.md](../07-health/sleep.md).

### Sleep Surface Safety Standards

**Federal standards -- what to look for when buying:**

All infant sleep products sold in the US must meet mandatory CPSC safety standards. The key ones:

| Product | Standard | Key Requirements |
|---------|----------|------------------|
| **Bassinets/cradles** | 16 CFR Part 1218 (incorporates ASTM F2194) | Firm flat surface, <=10 degree incline, structural integrity testing, small parts prohibition, entrapment prevention |
| **Full-size cribs** | 16 CFR Part 1219 (ASTM F1169) | Slat spacing <=2-3/8", no drop sides (banned 2011), hardware durability |
| **Non-full-size cribs (mini cribs)** | 16 CFR Part 1220 (ASTM F406) | Same safety principles as full-size, includes play yards |
| **Play yards** | 16 CFR Part 1221 (ASTM F406) | Mesh/fabric side integrity, folding mechanism locks, firm mattress pad |

(Source: [CPSC -- Bassinets and Cradles](https://www.cpsc.gov/FAQ/Bassinets-and-Cradles); [CPSC -- Safe Sleep](https://www.cpsc.gov/SafeSleep))

**What's banned:**
- *Evidence:* Inclined sleepers (>10 degree angle) are **banned** under the Safe Sleep for Babies Act (effective November 2022) and codified in a CPSC final rule (effective September 2023). This followed multiple infant deaths linked to products like the Fisher-Price Rock 'n Play. (Source: [Federal Register -- Ban of Inclined Sleepers for Infants](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2023/08/16/2023-17350/ban-of-inclined-sleepers-for-infants))
- Crib bumpers are also banned under the same law.
- As of May 2025, infant loungers/support cushions must meet a new mandatory CPSC safety standard -- they must be firm enough to prevent suffocation and have <=10 degree incline. (Source: [CPSC -- Infant Sleep Products Safety Standard](https://www.cpsc.gov/Newsroom/News-Releases/2022/CPSCs-New-Federal-Infant-Sleep-Products-Safety-Standard-Takes-Effect))

**2025-2026 recall surge -- baby loungers and off-brand sleep products:**

The CPSC has issued a wave of recalls and warnings in 2025-2026 targeting off-brand baby loungers and travel bassinets sold on Amazon, Walmart.com, and SHEIN. Common violations: sides too low to contain an infant, no stand (fall hazard on elevated surfaces), entrapment hazards.

- **Fasando baby lounger** -- infant death reported (2-month-old found unresponsive after being placed to sleep on lounger atop an adult bed). ~6,200 units sold on Amazon for ~$35. (Source: [CPSC -- Fasando Warning](https://www.cpsc.gov/Warnings/2026/CPSC-Warns-Consumers-to-Immediately-Stop-Usi-Fasando-Baby-Loungers-Due-to-Risk-of-Serious-Injury-or-Death-from-Fall-and-Entrapment-Hazards-Violates-Mandatory-Standard-for-Infant-Sleep-Products-Infant-Death-Reported))
- **Tuyedoqe travel bassinets** -- strangulation hazard from restraints, sides too low, no stand. ~250 units sold on Amazon for ~$40. Manufacturer refused to recall. (Source: [CPSC -- Tuyedoqe Warning](https://www.cpsc.gov/Warnings/2026/CPSC-Warns-Consumers-to-Immediately-Stop-Using-Tuyedoqe-Travel-Bassinets-Due-to-Risk-of-Serious-Injury-or-Death-from-Strangulation-and-Fall-Hazards-Violates-Mandatory-Standard-for-Infant-Sleep-Products))
- **Additional recalled brands** include Bearlala/Granlass, Alinux, Sofoliana, Glotika, YGJT, and XZV -- all priced $18-50 on marketplace sites.
- **Vevor baby swing** -- recalled for violating inclined sleeper ban, posing suffocation risk. (Source: [CPSC -- Vevor Baby Swing Recall](https://www.cpsc.gov/Recalls/2026/Sanven-Technology-Recalls-Vevor-Baby-Swings-Due-to-Risk-of-Serious-Injury-from-Suffocation-Hazard-Violates-Mandatory-Standard-for-Infant-Sleep-Products-and-Inclined-Sleepers-Ban))

> **Practical takeaway:** Buy sleep products from established brands (HALO, Arm's Reach, DaVinci, Graco, Chicco) sold by reputable retailers. The CPSC recall pattern is clear: cheap, unbranded products from third-party marketplace sellers are the problem. Check [CPSC recalls](https://www.cpsc.gov/Recalls) before buying, especially if buying used or from an unfamiliar brand. Our decided products (Arm's Reach / HALO bassinet, DaVinci Sammy Scallop crib) are from established manufacturers with no active recalls.

**Compact/travel bassinets -- caution:**
- The CPSC rejected a 2022 ASTM revision that would have allowed compact bassinets (no legs/stand). These products can be placed on beds or counters and fall, causing head injuries and deaths. If a bassinet doesn't have a freestanding base, it doesn't meet the federal standard. (Source: [CPSC -- Bassinet Standard Determination](https://www.cpsc.gov/content/cpsc-approves-new-federal-safety-standard-for-bassinets-and-cradles))

> **What Oster would say:** All products that pass CPSC standards meet the same safety floor. Spending more on a $400 bassinet vs. a $150 one doesn't buy measurably more safety -- it buys features (portability, aesthetics, vibration). The real safety variable is using the product correctly: firm flat surface, no added bedding, nothing in the sleep space.

For specific product comparisons, see [crib-bassinet.md](../02-gear/crib-bassinet.md).

### Sleep Environment: Temperature, Ventilation, and Fan Use

**This matters more for us -- July baby in an apartment.**

**Temperature:**
- *Evidence:* The AAP 2022 policy statement identifies overheating as a SIDS risk factor but does **not** specify an exact room temperature. The commonly cited 68-72 F (20-22 C) range comes from expert consensus, not a specific study establishing a threshold. The AAP's actual guidance: "dress infants appropriately for the environment, with no more than 1 layer more than an adult would wear." Watch for signs of overheating: sweating, flushed skin, chest hot to the touch. (Source: [AAP 2022 Evidence Base](https://publications.aap.org/pediatrics/article/150/1/e2022057991/188305/Evidence-Base-for-2022-Updated-Recommendations-for))
- *Evidence:* Babies do not sweat as much as adults and get hot more quickly, making them more prone to overheating and dehydration. HealthyChildren.org recommends fans or air conditioners to keep skin cool, and thin, loose-fitting cotton clothing for airflow. Avoid directing fan flow directly at a baby when room temperature exceeds 90 F -- dry blowing air can raise dehydration risk. (Source: [HealthyChildren.org -- Extreme Heat Tips](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/safety-prevention/at-home/Pages/Protecting-Children-from-Extreme-Heat-Information-for-Parents.aspx))
- *Evidence:* Emily Oster's take: the 68-72 F range is reasonable but not a hard cutoff. A room pushing 80 F is not ideal but "isn't worth staying up at night worrying about" -- dress baby lighter (just a diaper or single light layer), use a fan, and monitor for overheating signs. (Source: [ParentData -- What Temperature Should My Baby's Room Be?](https://parentdata.org/babies/is-it-dangerous-for-my-baby-to-sleep-in-a-hot-room/))

**Fan use:**
- *Evidence:* Coleman-Phox et al. (2008) found fan use during sleep was associated with a **72% reduction in SIDS risk** (adjusted OR 0.28, 95% CI 0.10-0.77). The effect was stronger in warmer rooms (AOR 0.06) and for non-supine sleepers (AOR 0.14). The proposed mechanism is that fans prevent CO2 pooling near the infant's face. (Source: [Coleman-Phox et al., *Arch Pediatr Adolesc Med* 2008;162(10):963-968](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18838649/))
- *Evidence quality:* Weak-moderate. This is a **single case-control study** (185 SIDS cases, 312 controls) with a 50% participation rate and potential recall bias. The AAP 2022 policy statement explicitly states "there is currently insufficient evidence to recommend the use of a fan as a SIDS risk-reduction strategy."
- *What Oster would say:* The fan study is suggestive but not definitive. However, the cost of running a fan is essentially zero (financially, practically, and in terms of risk), and for a July baby in an apartment that may run warm, a fan serves double duty -- temperature management and possible SIDS risk reduction. This is a low-cost, no-downside intervention.

**Fan placement for nursery nook:**
- Position the fan to circulate air in the room, **not blowing directly on baby** -- face it toward a wall or use oscillating mode aimed above crib level
- Tower fans are quieter than box fans and work well in small spaces; clip fans are another space-efficient option
- A ceiling fan, if available, is ideal -- no floor space used, good air circulation

**Summer baby checklist for our situation:**
- [ ] Get a simple room thermometer for the bedroom (~$10)
- [ ] Plan for a fan in the bedroom -- ceiling fan, standing fan, or clip fan positioned to circulate air (not blowing directly on baby)
- [ ] Stock lightweight sleep sacks (0.5 TOG or muslin) for summer -- see [essentials.md](../02-gear/essentials.md#swaddles--sleep-sacks) for specific picks (Burt's Bees 0.5 TOG or HALO cotton muslin 0.5 TOG)
- [ ] On very hot nights: just a diaper + light swaddle or sleep sack is fine
- [ ] Blackout curtains serve double duty -- blocking heat during the day and light for naps/bedtime
- [ ] If apartment has AC, set to a comfortable level; if no AC, consider a portable AC unit or window unit for the bedroom

### Blackout Curtains: Evidence for a Summer Baby

- *Evidence:* Infant circadian rhythm (melatonin-driven sleep-wake cycle) begins developing around **3-4 months**. Before that, newborns produce very little melatonin. Light exposure suppresses melatonin; darkness promotes it. A scoping review found that cycled lighting (bright days, dark nights) improved nighttime sleep consolidation and daytime wakefulness. (Source: [PMC -- Role of Light Exposure in Infant Circadian Rhythm](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11685245/))
- *Evidence:* Even 5 minutes of bright light exposure can delay the melatonin phase by up to 3 hours. For a July baby, summer daylight extends past 8 PM -- blackout curtains help signal "nighttime" when it's still bright outside.
- *Practical:* Blackout curtains also reduce room temperature by blocking solar heat gain -- dual benefit for a summer apartment.
- *Emily Oster's take:* Oster recommends trying blackout curtains, noting that a dark room promotes melatonin production. She cautions that children accustomed to total darkness may struggle sleeping in brighter environments (travel, grandparents' house), though she notes limited data on this. (Source: [ParentData -- Should I Hang Blackout Curtains?](https://parentdata.org/babies/blackout-curtains-babys-room/))

**Timing nuance:** For the first 6-8 weeks, **don't** use blackout curtains for daytime naps -- daytime light exposure helps establish the circadian rhythm. After ~3 months when melatonin production kicks in, darkening the room for naps and bedtime becomes helpful.

**Renter-friendly options:**
- Standard rod-hung blackout curtains (most common, works on existing curtain rod)
- Tension rod blackout curtains (no drilling, renter-friendly)
- Portable suction-cup blackout shades (also useful for travel)
- Consumer Reports tests confirm that even budget blackout curtains (Amazon Basics, Mainstays) effectively block light (Source: [Consumer Reports -- Best Blackout Curtains](https://www.consumerreports.org/home-garden/curtains/best-blackout-curtains-of-the-year-a3457628193/))

**For our apartment:** Install blackout curtains now (weeks 22-24). They immediately help with summer heat management even before baby arrives. After birth, keep them open during daytime for the first 6-8 weeks. After ~3 months, use them for naps and bedtime.

### Does a Baby Need a Dedicated Nursery?

**Short answer: No. And room-sharing is actually recommended.**

- *Evidence:* There is no research demonstrating that a dedicated nursery produces better infant developmental or sleep outcomes compared to a well-organized shared space. The AAP actively recommends room-sharing for the first 6 months, which means a separate nursery is unnecessary -- and potentially counterproductive -- during the highest-risk period. (Source: [AAP 2022 Policy Statement](https://publications.aap.org/pediatrics/article/150/1/e2022057990/188304/Sleep-Related-Infant-Deaths-Updated-2022))
- *Evidence:* For visual/cognitive development, infants need some visual stimulation during waking hours, but an overstimulating environment can cause stress. A "medium" level of visual interest during awake time is ideal -- this doesn't require a dedicated room. (Source: [Designing the Nursery: Environment and Infant Development](https://pressbooks.pub/hownurturematters/chapter/chapter-1/))
- *Opinion:* The cultural expectation that every baby "needs" a nursery is marketing-driven, not evidence-based. Babies need a safe sleep surface, responsive caregivers, and appropriate stimulation -- not a Pinterest-worthy room.

### Bassinet-to-Crib Transition

Most babies transition between **3-6 months**. The triggers are safety-based, not age-based:

**Immediate transition triggers (stop using bassinet):**
1. **Rolling over** -- once baby rolls in either direction, the bassinet is no longer safe (shallow sides, risk of climbing/falling). Most babies roll between 3-5 months.
2. **Exceeding weight limit** -- Arm's Reach Clear-Vue: ~20 lbs; HALO BassiNest 4.0: ~20 lbs. Check your specific product manual.
3. **Pushing up on hands and knees** -- risk of toppling over the side.
4. **Attempting to sit up** -- same fall risk.

**Soft transition signals:**
- Baby looks cramped, frequently wakes hitting the sides
- Disrupted sleep that improves in a larger space
- Baby outgrows the length even if under the weight limit

**Transition tips:**
- Start with naps in the crib before moving nighttime sleep
- Keep the sensory environment consistent -- same sound machine, same sleep sack, similar darkness level
- Expect 3-7 days of adjustment
- If transitioning to a crib in a different room, this can coincide with the room-sharing transition (after 6 months)

> **Planning note for our apartment:** The transition from bassinet to crib is when we execute the Phase 2 layout. The DaVinci Sammy Scallop needs ~54"x30" of floor space. Options: (a) replace the bassinet with the crib in the same bedroom spot, (b) set up the crib in the living area with a room divider, (c) closet conversion if dimensions allow. Having the crib purchased and stored by week 32-34 means it's ready to assemble when the time comes. Measure now so you're not problem-solving with a 5-month-old.

### Noise Machines: Evidence and Safety

**Do they work?**
- *Evidence:* A small RCT (Spencer et al., 1990) found 80% of newborns fell asleep within 5 minutes with white noise vs. 25% without (n=40). A 2025 meta-analysis of RCTs found white noise significantly increased total infant sleep time by ~137 minutes/day and reduced nighttime awakenings. (Sources: [Spencer et al., *Arch Dis Child* 1990](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/2405784/); [Ding et al., 2025 meta-analysis](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41151421/))
- *Evidence quality:* Moderate. Individual studies are small, but the meta-analysis of multiple RCTs shows consistent benefit.

**Are they safe?**
- *Evidence:* A 2014 AAP-affiliated study tested 14 infant sound machines and found **all** exceeded the 50 dB recommended limit for hospital nurseries when at maximum volume at close range. At max volume, 9 of 14 exceeded 85 dB (NIOSH occupational limit) at 10 cm distance. However, **no device exceeded 85 dB at 30 cm or 100 cm distance** at any volume. (Source: [Hugh et al., *Pediatrics* 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24616357/))
- *Evidence:* The AAP's 2023 noise exposure report recommends: place sound machines **at least 7 feet (200 cm) from the infant**, set volume **as low as possible**, and **limit duration of use**. The practical consensus is **<=50 dB at the baby's ear** -- roughly the volume of a quiet conversation. (Source: [AAP -- Preventing Excessive Noise Exposure 2023](https://publications.aap.org/pediatrics/article/152/5/e2023063752/194468/Preventing-Excessive-Noise-Exposure-in-Infants))
- *Evidence:* A 2024 scoping review flagged that continuous moderate-intensity (60-80 dB) white noise may affect auditory development in animal models. Human data is limited but suggests caution with high-volume continuous use. (Source: [De Jong et al., *Sleep Medicine* 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38663282/))

**Practical safe-use rules for our nursery nook:**
- Place machine at least 7 feet from baby's head (across the room, not in the crib) -- on the dresser or a far shelf works well
- Use the **lowest effective volume** -- you should be able to talk over it comfortably
- Use a free decibel meter app (NIOSH Sound Level Meter) to verify <=50 dB at the crib
- Consider using on a timer or turning off after baby falls asleep
- Product picks: see [essentials.md](../02-gear/essentials.md) -- Yogasleep Dohm (~$35-45) for home, Yogasleep Hushh (~$30) for portable

> **What Oster would say:** White noise works for helping babies sleep. The hearing risk is entirely about volume and distance -- not about the concept of white noise itself. A machine at low volume across the room is within safe parameters. The practical benefit (better sleep for baby AND parents) is meaningful; the risk at appropriate volume/distance is negligible.

### Cat Safety for Sleep Space

With 2 cats, the sleep zone needs physical barriers. Cats are attracted to warm, enclosed, soft spaces -- which is exactly what a bassinet is.

**Options ranked by situation fit for shared bedroom:**

| Barrier | Cat Effectiveness | Situation Fit |
|---------|------------------|---------------|
| **Bassinet with mesh sides + breathable cover** | Good (blocks access to sleep surface) | **Excellent** -- protects sleep surface without excluding cats from room |
| **Close bedroom door at night, cats elsewhere** | Excellent (100%) | **Fair** -- requires retraining cats who currently sleep with you; start weeks in advance |
| **Deterrents (foil, tape) around bassinet** | Moderate (training aid) | **Good** as supplementary strategy |
| **Crib net/tent** | Moderate | **Do not use** -- CPSC recalled 330,000 units; entanglement/strangulation risk exceeds cat risk |

**Our decided approach:** The best combination for room-sharing with cats is: (1) a bassinet with full mesh sides that make it physically awkward for cats (both the Arm's Reach Clear-Vue and HALO BassiNest have mesh sides), (2) add a breathable mesh cover from a reputable brand (~$30-40, check CPSC recalls), and (3) start deterrent training 4-6 weeks before due date. Alternatively, retrain cats to sleep outside the bedroom -- start by week 22-24 to give 16+ weeks of adjustment time.

See [cat-prep.md](cat-prep.md) for the full cat preparation plan, including the bedroom transition strategy and Feliway evidence.
See [baby-proofing.md](baby-proofing.md) for furniture anchoring in the nursery zone.
