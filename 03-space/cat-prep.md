# Cat Prep — Introducing Cats to Baby

> **Start:** Week 24-26 (gradual introduction to changes)
> **Note:** We have 2 cats — recommendations below account for managing both.

## Before Baby Arrives

### Gradual Changes (Start Now)
- [ ] Start playing baby sounds (crying, cooing) at low volume, gradually increase
- [ ] Introduce baby gear (swing, bassinet) early so both cats can investigate and get bored
- [ ] Set up baby's sleep space and establish it as off-limits to both cats
- [ ] If changing any cat routines (feeding spots, litter locations), do it NOW — not after baby arrives
- [ ] Practice carrying a doll/blanket while interacting with cats
- [ ] Ensure each cat has their own resources (food bowl, water, litter box) plus one extra — the "N+1 rule" for multi-cat households becomes more important during transitions

### Sleep Space Safety
- [ ] **Critical: Neither cat should have access to baby's sleep space** — see Research section for actual risk data
- [ ] Options:
  - ~~Crib net/tent~~ (recalled by CPSC — see Research section; do not use)
  - Close bedroom door + video monitor (best option for our shared bedroom setup)
  - Screen door on bedroom (allows cats to see in; reduces anxiety from exclusion)
  - Keep cats out of bedroom entirely (hardest adjustment if they currently sleep with us)
- [ ] Test your chosen barrier before baby arrives — start weeks in advance so cats adjust

### Scent Introduction
- [ ] Bring home a blanket or hat baby wore in hospital before bringing baby home
- [ ] Let each cat sniff and investigate at their own pace
- [ ] Reward calm behavior with treats (counterconditioning)

## When Baby Comes Home

- Greet the cats first (without baby) — one parent goes in ahead
- Let each cat approach baby on their own terms; don't force interaction
- Supervise all interactions
- Maintain both cats' routines as much as possible (feeding times, play, attention)
- Give cats "escape routes" — high perches, separate room, vertical space
- With 2 cats, watch for redirected aggression between them (one cat stressed about baby may lash out at the other)

## Ongoing

- **Never leave either cat and baby unsupervised** (until child is old enough to interact safely)
- Watch for stress signs in both cats: hiding, appetite changes, litter box issues, aggression, over-grooming
- Secure cat food, water, and litter boxes away from baby once mobile (~6 months)
- Consider Feliway diffuser if either cat seems stressed (see Research section for evidence)
- If cats show inter-cat tension from the stress, consider adding Feliway MultiCat diffuser (see Research section)
- Most cats adjust within 2-8 weeks; if stress signs persist beyond a few weeks, consult your vet

### 1. Cat Suffocation Risk — Myth vs. Evidence

> **Bottom line:** The "cats suffocate babies" fear is largely a myth rooted in centuries of folklore. The real (tiny) risk is accidental airway obstruction from a cat lying near a baby's face for warmth — not intentional harm. Standard safe-sleep practices (bare crib, no loose items, keep pets off sleep surface) address this adequately.

*Evidence:* The medical literature contains essentially **one documented near-miss case** (1982, *British Medical Journal*: a 5-week-old found with a cat lying over her face; resuscitated successfully). A broader search found only four suspected cases since 1980, and for three of them the only evidence was the cat being in the same room — indistinguishable from coincidental SIDS. (Source: [Sage & Woodward, "Can a cat smother and kill a baby?" BMJ 1982](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1499543/))

*Evidence:* A 1971 article in *Pediatrics* (AAP) noted that in all the research into SIDS etiology, mechanical suffocation by a cat was never implicated as a risk factor. (Source: ["On Cats and Crib Death," Pediatrics 1971](https://publications.aap.org/pediatrics/article/47/3/515/46497/ON-CATS-AND-CRIB-DEATH))

*Evidence:* The most publicized case (UK, 2000) — a 6-week-old found dead with the family cat on his face — was determined by autopsy to be SIDS. The cat's proximity was coincidental. (Source: [Live Science](https://www.livescience.com/22449-do-cats-really-kill-babies-by-sucking-away-their-breath.html))

*Evidence:* The AAP 2022 safe sleep guidelines explicitly list **pets** among items that should not be on or near an infant's sleep surface, alongside pillows, blankets, and loose bedding. This is a general airway-obstruction precaution, not a cat-specific finding. (Source: [AAP 2022 Safe Sleep Recommendations](https://publications.aap.org/pediatrics/article/150/1/e2022057990/188304/Sleep-Related-Infant-Deaths-Updated-2022))

**Oster framework analysis:**
- **Actual measured risk:** Near zero. Fewer than 5 suspected cases in 40+ years of medical literature, none conclusively attributed to cat suffocation.
- **Comparison:** SIDS itself affects ~0.33 per 1,000 live births (~1 in 3,000). The risk of a cat specifically causing suffocation is orders of magnitude lower than SIDS, which is itself a rare event.
- **Cost of mitigation:** Low. Keeping cats off the sleep surface is straightforward with a closed door or physical barrier — interventions you would already use for general safe sleep.
- **Verdict:** The precaution (keeping cats away from sleep surface) is sensible and low-cost, but the fear level should be proportionate. This is not the safety issue that should keep you up at night.

### 2. Crib Nets/Tents — CPSC Recall and Safety Evidence

> **Bottom line:** Do not use crib tents or aftermarket crib nets. They were recalled by the CPSC after a child death and serious injuries, and they are an unregulated product category. The entanglement risk from the tent itself is more dangerous than the cat access it's trying to prevent.

*Evidence:* In 2012, the CPSC recalled approximately **330,000 Tots in Mind crib tents** after reports of **1 death** (2-year-old in 2008) and **1 catastrophic brain injury** (2-year-old in 2007, broken rod trapped child at the neck), plus 27 total tent failure incidents. The manufacturer went out of business. (Source: [CPSC Recall Notice](https://www.cpsc.gov/Recalls/2012/five-retailers-agree-to-stop-sale-and-recall-tots-in-mind-crib-tents-due-to))

*Evidence:* Crib tents fall outside the Consumer Product Safety Improvement Act of 2008, which established standards for 12 categories of durable juvenile products. There is **no federal safety standard** for aftermarket crib nets or mesh covers. (Source: [Safety Research & Strategies](https://www.safetyresearch.net/crib-tents-another-hazard-from-the-world-of-unregulated-child-products/))

*Evidence:* The CPSC has documented at least 10 incidents involving crib tents or canopies in which a child was entangled in mesh, the dome inverted, or the child tore a hole and became entrapped. Hazards include: dome inversion trapping infant inside, partial detachment creating entrapment gaps, and broken support rods at neck level. (Source: [CPSC](https://www.cpsc.gov/Recalls/2012/five-retailers-agree-to-stop-sale-and-recall-tots-in-mind-crib-tents-due-to))

**Oster framework analysis:**
- **Risk of crib tent:** Documented deaths and serious injuries from the product itself. Unregulated, no safety standard.
- **Risk it's mitigating:** Cat suffocation of infant — near-zero documented risk (see above).
- **Verdict:** The "solution" (crib tent) has caused more documented harm than the problem (cat suffocation) it claims to solve. A closed door or screen door is safer in every way.

### 3. Sleep Space Barrier Options — Comparison for Our Situation

Since we're room-sharing (no separate nursery), our barrier options are different from families with a dedicated nursery. The barrier needs to keep 2 cats off the sleep surface in a room where we also sleep.

| Barrier Option | Cat Effectiveness | Safe Sleep Compatible | Fits Our Situation | Cost | Situation Fit |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Closed bedroom door + video monitor** | Excellent (100%) | Yes | **Poor** — locks us out of our own bedroom, or locks cats in with us | $30-80 (monitor) | **Poor** for shared bedroom |
| **Close bedroom door at night, cats sleep elsewhere** | Excellent (100%) | Yes | **Fair** — requires retraining cats who currently sleep with us; start weeks before baby arrives | $0 + monitor | **Fair** — free but big adjustment for 2 cats |
| **Bassinet with closing canopy/cover** | Good (blocks access to sleep surface specifically) | Check product is CPSC-rated for sleep | **Good** — protects sleep surface without excluding cats from entire room | Varies by bassinet model | **Excellent** if bassinet has integrated cover |
| **Screen door on bedroom** | Good (cat can see in, reduces stress) | Yes | **Poor** — we sleep in this room too; a screen door doesn't help for room-sharing | ~$50-150 installed | **Poor** for shared bedroom |
| **Extra-tall cat gate (72")** | Very good (even jumpers can't clear) | Yes | **Poor** — same problem as screen door for room-sharing | ~$50-100 | **Poor** for shared bedroom |
| ~~**Crib net/tent**~~ | ~~Moderate~~ | ~~**NO — recalled by CPSC, entanglement/strangulation risk**~~ | ~~N/A~~ | ~~$60-85~~ | ~~**Do not use**~~ |
| **Closed bassinet + keep cats off with training/deterrents** | Moderate (depends on cat persistence) | Yes | **Good** — aluminum foil or double-sided tape on/around bassinet during training period | ~$5-10 | **Good** as supplementary strategy |

> **Our situation:** Because we're room-sharing with no separate nursery, the best options are: (1) a bassinet model with an integrated cover/canopy that blocks cat access to the sleep surface directly, or (2) training cats to sleep outside the bedroom before baby arrives and using a closed door + monitor. Option 1 lets cats stay in the room (less stressful for them); option 2 is the most foolproof. Consider combining: close bedroom door at night + use a bassinet with cover as backup. See [crib/bassinet notes](../02-gear/crib-bassinet.md) for models with covers.

> **For partner discussion:** Do we want to retrain both cats to sleep outside our bedroom before July, or invest in a bassinet with a cat-proof cover and let them stay? Starting the transition now (~20 weeks out) gives plenty of time either way.

### 4. Feliway — Evidence Assessment

> **Bottom line:** Feliway has moderate scientific support — better than placebo in multiple RCTs, but not a miracle product. For our situation (2 cats, major household disruption), the cost of trying it is low (~$25-35/month) and the downside risk is essentially zero. Reasonable to use as one part of a transition plan, not as a standalone fix.

*Evidence:* A meta-analysis found Feliway Classic more effective than placebo for reducing urine spraying, with >90% of cats showing reduction. (Source: [Feliway Published Clinical Studies](https://www.feliway.co.uk/pages/clinically-proven))

*Evidence:* A systematic review of 5 studies (including 3 double-blinded RCTs) rated the evidence for Feliway reducing acute stress as **moderate**, with 4 of 5 studies showing improvement in select stress indicators. (Source: [Veterinary Evidence, 2023](https://veterinaryevidence.org/index.php/ve/article/view/669))

*Evidence:* A large RCT (n=1,060 cat-caregiver pairs) found Feliway Classic reduced undesirable scratching in 83.5% of cats vs. 68.5% with placebo (p<0.0001) — a real but modest effect over placebo. (Source: [PLOS ONE, 2023](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0292188))

*Evidence:* For multi-cat households specifically, a double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled pilot trial (n=45 households) found Feliway MultiCat (aka Feliway Friends) significantly reduced inter-cat aggression by day 21 vs. placebo, with effects persisting 2 weeks post-treatment. 84% of Feliway households reported improvement vs. 64% placebo. (Source: [DePorter et al., JFMS 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6435919/))

**Caveats:**
- Several studies are funded by Ceva (Feliway's manufacturer) — conflict of interest is real
- Notable placebo effects in most studies (~60-70% improvement in control groups too)
- Evidence is strongest for spraying/scratching; less studied for generalized household transition stress
- Should be combined with behavioral modification, not used alone

**For our 2-cat household:**
- Feliway Classic (stress from environmental change) — plug in ~2 weeks before baby arrives
- Feliway MultiCat (inter-cat tension) — add if cats start redirecting stress at each other after baby arrives
- Cost: ~$25-35/diffuser/month. Low cost of trying, zero downside risk, moderate evidence of benefit.

### 5. Sound Desensitization — Evidence Assessment

> **Bottom line:** Desensitization and counterconditioning (DS/CC) are the standard veterinary behavioral recommendation. Evidence is stronger in dogs than cats, but the technique is well-established in feline behavior practice. Low cost, low risk, worth doing.

*Evidence:* Systematic desensitization and counterconditioning are the primary techniques used by feline behavior professionals to change a cat's emotional response to specific triggers (sounds, strangers, other animals). The approach: start at very low intensity, pair with positive stimulus (treats), gradually increase. (Source: [VCA Animal Hospitals](https://vcahospitals.com/know-your-pet/introduction-to-desensitization-and-counterconditioning))

*Evidence:* The Merck Veterinary Manual lists desensitization among the core behavior modification techniques for cats, alongside habituation, extinction, and counterconditioning. (Source: [Merck Veterinary Manual — Cat Behavior](https://www.merckvetmanual.com/cat-owners/behavior-of-cats/treatment-of-behavior-problems-in-cats))

*Evidence:* Veterinary sources note that some cats may react aggressively to baby crying sounds (which can resemble cat-fight vocalizations), and that this aggression can be redirected at the nearest target — another pet or person. This is especially relevant with 2 cats, as one stressed cat may redirect aggression at the other. (Source: [dvm360](https://www.dvm360.com/view/ensuring-behaviorally-healthy-pet-child-relationship))

**Limitations:**
- *Opinion:* Noise aversion in cats is poorly understood compared to dogs; the incidence is unknown
- Not all cats respond to recorded sounds — test first at real-life volume before committing to a multi-week program
- Risk of sensitization (making it worse) if pushed too fast

**Practical approach for 2 cats:**
1. Play baby-cry recordings at barely audible volume while cats eat or get treats
2. Watch both cats — if either shows signs of distress (ears back, hiding, hissing), lower volume
3. Gradually increase over 4-6 weeks
4. If one cat is unbothered and the other is reactive, you may need separate sessions

### 6. Scent Introduction — Evidence Assessment

> **Bottom line:** Widely recommended by veterinary behaviorists and based on sound principles of feline olfaction and scent-based communication. No controlled studies specifically for baby-to-cat introduction, but the underlying science is solid.

*Evidence:* Cats have approximately 200 million olfactory receptors (~1,000x more sensitive than humans). Scent is their primary method of gathering information about their environment. The scent-exchange technique draws on the same principle used in all cat-to-cat introductions. (Source: [International Cat Care](https://icatcare.org/articles/introducing-cats-and-dogs))

*Opinion:* Multiple veterinary practices and animal behavior organizations recommend sending home a baby-worn blanket for cats to investigate before baby arrives. Specific techniques include rubbing the blanket on cat furniture, placing it in high-traffic areas, and rewarding calm investigation with treats. (Source: [The Feline Hospital](https://thefelinehospital.com/babies-toddlers-and-cats-toxoplasmosis-marking-anxiety/), [ASPCA](https://www.aspca.org/pet-care/cat-care/cats-and-babies), [Small Door Veterinary](https://www.smalldoorvet.com/learning-center/behavior/bringing-your-new-baby-home-to-your-pet/))

**For 2 cats:** Let each cat investigate the scent blanket at their own pace. Place it in a neutral area — not near either cat's preferred resting spot. Don't force it; some cats won't care, and that's fine.

### 7. Post-Birth Cat Adjustment Timeline

*Evidence:* Most cats begin to adjust within **2-8 weeks**, though complete adjustment can take several months. Confident, social cats may show acceptance within days; anxious cats may need longer. (Source: [PetsCare — Veterinary behaviorist Valli Parthasarathy, PhD, DVM, DACVB](https://www.petscare.com/news/post/helping-cats-adjust-new-baby))

**Stress signs to watch for in both cats:**
- Hiding more than usual
- Appetite changes (eating less or more)
- Litter box avoidance or changes
- Excessive grooming (bald patches)
- Aggression toward each other (redirected stress)
- Vocalizing more than usual

**When to call the vet:**
- Stress signs persist beyond 2-3 weeks without improvement
- Either cat stops eating for more than 24-48 hours
- Aggression toward baby (growling, swatting, hissing near baby)
- Aggression between the 2 cats escalates beyond normal spats
- Consider consulting a board-certified veterinary behaviorist (DACVB) or Certified Applied Animal Behaviorist (CAAB) if standard approaches aren't working

*Anecdotal:* Many parents report that cats who were obsessed with the crib/bassinet before birth wanted nothing to do with it once a noisy baby was actually in it. Cats often self-select to give the baby space.

### 8. AAFP Guidelines and Multi-Cat Considerations

*Evidence:* The 2024 AAFP Intercat Tension Guidelines found that in a survey of 2,492 multi-cat households, **87.7% reported signs of intercat tension**. 73.3% said tension started with the introduction of a new household member. Key recommendation: avoid punishment-based responses; use reward-based training only. (Source: [Rodan et al., JFMS 2024](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1098612X241263465))

**Why 2 cats matters:** A new baby is a novel sensory stimulus that can trigger intercat tension even between cats that previously got along well. The AAFP guidelines specifically note that novel stimuli — whether a new cat or any major environmental change — can be stressful for resident cats. With 2 cats, you're managing:
1. Each cat's individual adjustment to the baby
2. The relationship between the 2 cats under stress
3. The "N+1" resource rule becomes critical — 3 litter boxes (2 cats + 1), separate feeding stations, multiple resting spots at different heights
