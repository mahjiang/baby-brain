# Formula — Backup Knowledge

> Even if planning to breastfeed, it's smart to know the basics. No judgment — fed is best.

## When You Might Need Formula

- Supplementing if milk supply is low or slow to come in (common in first days)
- If breastfeeding doesn't work out
- If birthing parent needs medication incompatible with breastfeeding
- Combo feeding (breast milk + formula) — increasingly common and totally fine

## What to Know

- All formula sold in the US must meet FDA nutritional standards — generic/store brand is nutritionally equivalent to name brand
- Types: powder (cheapest), liquid concentrate, ready-to-feed (most convenient, most expensive)
- Ready-to-feed is recommended for newborns in the first days/weeks (sterile, no mixing)
- **Do not** make homemade formula or dilute formula

## Recommended Brands (All FDA-Regulated)

| Type | Examples | Notes |
|------|---------|-------|
| Standard cow's milk-based | Enfamil NeuroPro, Similac 360 Total Care, generic equivalents | Most babies do fine on standard |
| Gentle / reduced lactose | Enfamil Gentlease, Similac Total Comfort | For gassy/fussy babies |
| Hypoallergenic | Enfamil Nutramigen, Similac Alimentum | For confirmed milk protein allergy (pediatrician-directed) |
| Soy-based | Enfamil ProSobee, Similac Soy Isomil | Less commonly recommended now |

## What to Buy (Just in Case)

- [ ] One small container of ready-to-feed newborn formula (2 oz bottles)
- [ ] A couple of slow-flow nipples / bottles
- Don't stockpile — you can always buy more; babies may prefer specific brands
